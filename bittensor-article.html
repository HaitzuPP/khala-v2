Executive Summary

Artificial Intelligence development is increasingly centralized, closed, and extractive. A handful of frontier labs (OpenAI, Google, Anthropic, Meta) control the most capable models, the data pipelines, the compute infrastructure, and the economic upside.

Bittensor's answer: intelligence as commodity: openly priced, competitively produced, cryptographically verified.

The network now has paying customers:

  • Signed enterprise contracts: Reading FC, 3,000+ AVIA petrol stations, $10B+ market cap payments companies (under NDA).
  • Institutional capital entering: Grayscale Trust products, DCG's Yuma receiving financing from Connecticut Innovations, TAO ETPs.
  • Academic validation: Templar's SparseLoCo accepted at NeurIPS OPT2025, the first Bittensor-originating research at a major ML conference.
  • Technical milestones: Largest permissionless pre-training run ever conducted (Covenant 72B).

Real customers. Real money. Asymmetric upside.

01

The Concentration Problem and Crypto's Solution

Each generation of large language models has cost more to train. In 2020, OpenAI trained GPT-3 for roughly $5M, the best language model in the world. Today, frontier models range from $500M to over $1B. The barrier to competitive AI compounds every year, in favor of incumbents.

For investors: private, priced into mega-caps, or routing through a single chipmaker.

Five companies control the foundation models that will underpin the next decade of software. Between them, they command the talent pipelines, the GPU allocations, the data partnerships, and the distribution. Everyone else builds on their infrastructure, at their margins, on their terms. Bittensor is trying to change the rules.

Bittensor is no longer a bet on whether. It's a bet on how large.

Crypto was supposed to be good at this, permissionless coordination, global capital formation, incentive alignment without gatekeepers. For years, it wasn't. Decentralized compute projects struggled to generate revenue. AI agent frameworks came and went.

That's starting to change. Decentralized training compute is now growing significantly faster than centralized infrastructure. Bittensor is leading that shift: token incentives disaggregate AI development entirely. Instead of one lab training one model, a global network of miners contributes specialized capabilities and gets paid based on output.

  • Permissionless participation: anyone can contribute if they deliver.
  • Continuous competition: underperformers lose emissions to those who produce.
  • Token-based capital efficiency: no Series C required to scale.

Over the past year, the network matured. Dynamic TAO made subnets directly investable. The first halving tightened supply. Multiple subnets crossed from pilots to contracts. Institutional capital followed: Grayscale's ETF filing, Deutsche Digital Assets' ETP, DCG-backed Yuma launching a subnet fund.

  • Pre-training builds foundation models. Capital-intensive. Highest potential moat.

Computer vision is one of the largest addressable markets in AI. Billions of cameras generate footage every day, but extracting intelligence from video still demands proprietary hardware or expensive cloud compute from Google, Nvidia, and AWS. A single football match still costs hundreds of dollars to annotate.

SCORE's Manako AI converts live video into structured, queryable datasets, comparable to Google's AutoML or Nvidia's Cosmos VLM, but with a key structural difference: edge cases route to SN44's decentralized miner network, where miners compete to expand model capabilities and earn TAO. Each new customer deployment surfaces new edge cases. Each edge case sharpens the miner network. Quality compounds with usage, not headcount.

100% trial conversion (self-reported) Multi-vertical traction ARR not disclosed Cost claims self-reported, unaudited

No competitor currently offers a decentralized, incentive-driven vision platform spanning multiple verticals at SCORE's claimed price point. Google AutoML and Amazon Rekognition are image-first and scale costs linearly per call. Nvidia Cosmos operates at a different stack layer entirely.

Verdict Real product, real customers, active miner network. The flywheel is turning. Vision AI is early and fragmented, no dominant platform has emerged for turning the world's camera infrastructure into intelligent data streams.

Financial institutions spend billions annually on compliance systems rarely tested under real attack conditions. Most institutions can't prove their defenses actually work. SN54 MIID (Multimodal Inorganic Identities Dataset) by Yanez Compliance has pre-existing enterprise customers and fiat revenue, yet has received minimal attention from the broader TAO community.

Incubated by Yuma, backed by a $900K seed with Deep Ventures, MIID generates synthetic identities and adversarial scenarios to stress-test KYC, AML, and digital identity defenses. Miners produce ID variations (name transliterations, deepfake biometrics); validators reward miners based on bypass success, detection latency, and adversarial realism. The output is sold to financial institutions to harden their defenses.

Regulatory spend, sticky in downturns Unique adversarial positioning Pipeline clients un-named Revenue reinvestment not published

The compliance data market is dominated by slow, centralized vendors. LSEG/Refinitiv World-Check and LexisNexis update periodically, meaning there's always a gap between a new sanctions evasion technique and the response. MIID attacks the systems rather than running detection. That distinction matters.

Verdict Real enterprise traction in a lucrative, sticky market. The challenge: converting the pipeline and scaling relationships. $900K is thin for enterprise sales into regulated finance. Watch for the next fundraise.

Drug discovery: 90%+ of candidates fail. Costs exceed $2.6B and 10 years to market. Metanova (SN68) crowdsources molecular design through Bittensor, Recursion or Insilico, but open-sourced and token-incentivized.

Two competition formats: Compound challenges (miners submit candidate molecules for defined protein targets) and Blueprint challenges (miners submit search algorithms exploring chemical space more efficiently). Both create a feedback loop, better algorithms improve the molecule discovery engine over time. Near-zero marginal screening cost vs. centralized R&D burning millions.

MOU with Yalotein Bio (Yale PhD-led) Near-zero marginal screening cost Wet-lab validation Q1–Q2 2026 No revenue; licensing path unclear

Centralized competition is well-funded: Recursion ($2.1B market cap), Insilico ($500M+ raised), Isomorphic Labs (DeepMind, $3B in pharma partnerships). NOVA's structural advantage is cost and throughput, if wet-lab validation lands.

Verdict A $1.5T+ industry with a structurally broken pipeline. The risk is real, wet-lab validation is expensive, and the path from virtual screening to therapeutic candidate is long. The upside is asymmetric. Highest variance bet of the five.

Frontier model pre-training is increasingly concentrated. The compute required doubles every 6–10 months, pricing out anyone but the major labs. Templar is emerging as the most credible decentralized pre-training platform, and uniquely, it's permissionless. Prime Intellect, Nous, and Pluralis all curate or gate participants. Templar doesn't.

SparseLoCo compresses gradients by ~97% while outperforming uncompressed baselines, enabling 72B parameter training over the public internet with just 6% communication overhead. The Templar-1B run (20,000+ cycles, ~200 GPUs) matches centralized baselines across HellaSwag, PIQA, and ARC-E. At first checkpoint, Covenant 72B outperforms all completed decentralized pre-training runs on measured benchmarks.

Only permissionless training in production NeurIPS acceptance, first Bittensor ecosystem paper 72B final benchmarks pending ~300× smaller than frontier datacenters

The revenue flywheel: 100% of training-as-a-service fees fund Covenant token buybacks, linking commercial demand directly to miner incentives. Full-stack coverage across three subnets (pre-training, compute, post-training/RL) gives Templar vertical integration competitors lack. Epoch AI data: decentralized training compute grows 4× faster than centralized infrastructure.

Verdict The thesis hinges on Covenant 72B final benchmarks. If the model matches or exceeds centralized baselines at equivalent scale, decentralized pre-training is validated. The macro tailwind is clear, the gap narrows significantly within 2–3 years at current growth rates.

AI inference is where value gets captured. Training builds the model; inference is where it earns money. Global inference market: $116B in 2025, growing 17–19% CAGR toward $250–540B by 2030–2034. Chutes is an open-source, decentralized compute provider for deploying and running open-source models in production, and publishes a live revenue dashboard, making it one of the most transparent revenue subnets in crypto.

Revenue breakdown: 55% pay-as-you-go, 25% sponsored inference, 5% subscribers, 5% bounties. Pay-as-you-go dominating signals genuine developer usage, not marketing subsidies. But 25% sponsored means the organic-to-sponsored ratio is the key metric to watch. Chutes ranks as OpenRouter's #1 provider and consistently offers newly released open-source models first (DeepSeek V3, Kimi K2), driving developer trial and sticky usage.

#1 provider on OpenRouter First-mover on new model releases 25% sponsored revenue, watch ratio Single validator concentration (Rayon Labs)

The real moat is the incentive-aligned supply side. Miners earn through TAO emissions, creating a supply floor that VC-funded competitors can't match without continued fundraising or margin compression. For context: OpenRouter processes ~1T tokens/day. Chutes runs ~100B–120B tokens/day, with 20–25% flowing through OpenRouter.

Verdict $5.5M annualized is modest against centralized providers, but it's the strongest revenue signal in Bittensor. The central question: does the incentive-aligned supply side translate into a durable moat, or get overwhelmed by VC-backed competitors racing to the price floor?

Closing Thesis

The five subnets in this report span inference compute, computer vision, regulatory compliance, drug discovery, and model pre-training. What they share is a structural advantage that centralized competitors cannot replicate: token-incentivized competition that compounds output quality with usage, not headcount.

We're not naive about the gap between early traction and sustainable commercial scale. This report profiles 5 out of 128 subnets. Not all will succeed. But the ones generating revenue, signing enterprise contracts, and passing academic peer review are no longer experiments, these are early-stage AI companies with an unusual cost structure and a novel go-to-market.

Today, centralized AI labs collectively command valuations above $1 trillion. TAO's fully diluted valuation sits at approximately $4 billion, 1/125th of OpenAI alone. The question has shifted. It's no longer whether decentralized AI can work. These subnets are showing it can.
