Market Context

Robotics is at the same inflection point that cloud computing was in 2010 — the hardware commoditisation curve has started, the software race has begun, and most of the infrastructure value hasn't been built yet.

The global robotics market stands at approximately $128B today and is forecast to reach $275B by 2030, driven by falling actuator costs, maturing vision-language-action (VLA) models, and national industrial policy tailwinds in the US, China, EU, and South Korea. Goldman Sachs independently projects the humanoid segment alone reaches $38B within a decade — comparable to the entire current EV battery supply chain.

What's different about this cycle is the convergence of four previously separate technology curves: AI inference becoming cheap enough to run on-device, robotic hardware cost per degree-of-freedom falling 20–30% annually (consistent with EV motor and actuator deflation), micropayment infrastructure (x402, USDC settlements) making machine-to-machine transactions viable, and decentralised physical infrastructure providing the coordination layer that no single hardware vendor will build.

The traditional robotics incumbents — Boston Dynamics, ABB, FANUC, Teradyne — are hardware companies. Their moats are in mechanics, manufacturing, and service contracts. The software, positioning, spatial context, and economic coordination layers are wide open. That's the opportunity mapped in this report.

GEODNET's network of 20,000+ satellite receiver stations provides RTK (Real-Time Kinematic) corrections across 150+ countries, enabling the precision required for drone delivery, autonomous agricultural machinery, and mobile robotics in complex environments.

What separates GEODNET from every other DePIN project is that it already has a working revenue model. $7M ARR with compounding quarterly growth, an 80% token buyback mechanism tied directly to fee revenue, and an A16z DePIN report ranking it second behind Helium for category size. Auki's 'Posemesh' technology builds a shared, persistent 3D map of the world that multiple robots and AR devices can reference simultaneously. This "Real World Web" eliminates the need for each robot to maintain its own spatial model — enabling multi-robot collaboration, object permanence, and navigation without centralised data servers. The privacy architecture is edge-first: encrypted clusters with local computation, no global data sharing.

The enterprise traction is the differentiator. $2M+ ARR from Swedish supermarket COOP trials, active pilots with Toyota Material Handling, Unitree, Padbot, Slamtec, and EngineAI. The results speak: 30% reduction in walking distance for staff, 15 min/day handover time savings, and 4,000m² store mapping completed in 4 hours versus days previously. These are real operational metrics from paying enterprise customers — not user count vanity metrics.

Auki took a DEX-first approach to build community before a CEX listing, which is planned for 2026. The token burn mechanism (credits purchased by burning $AUKI) creates direct demand tied to network usage.

The Composability Advantage

The strategic edge of decentralised robotics infrastructure is not individual protocol performance — it's the composability between layers that creates network effects no single company can replicate.

Protocol Stack Example
PEAQ (identity + payments) + GEODNET (centimetre positioning) + Auki (shared spatial map) = autonomous, location-aware robotic fleets that settle in real-time without centralised infrastructure

CodecFLOW and OpenMind are prime examples of hardware-agnostic SDKs that any robotics operator can plug into — eliminating the lock-in of proprietary APIs (the "Apptronik SDK" or "Figure SDK"). Once a robot is running OM1 and plugged into CodecFLOW, it gains access to 450+ VLA models through 1,200+ compute nodes, GEODNET positioning, and Auki spatial context — all settled via PEAQ or x402 micropayments.

This is the flywheel that traditional robotics companies cannot build: open, composable infrastructure where each protocol benefits from the others scaling. Boston Dynamics, ABB, and FANUC are optimised for their own hardware ecosystems. The decentralised layer wins by being hardware-neutral.

Investment Outlook

DeRobotics tokens trade at sub-$1B valuations against traditional robotics equities in the tens of billions — same market exposure, fraction of the price, with compressing hardware cost curves acting as the re-rating catalyst.

The projects covered in this report constitute approximately $200M (27%) of the total DeRobotics segment market cap. The gap versus traditional robotics equities is driven by hardware: companies like ABB, FANUC, and Boston Dynamics have tangible capital tied up in manufacturing, supply chains, and service contracts. The software and coordination layers have structurally lower capital requirements and higher gross margins — but markets haven't priced that asymmetry yet.

BULL — 3 Years

Humanoid cost-per-unit falls below $20K. Enterprise adoption follows consumer EV adoption curve. GEODNET and Auki become default infrastructure for robotic fleets. XMAQUINA portfolio IPOs. TAO robotic subnets reach $50M+ combined ARR.

BASE — 3 Years

Industrial robotics leads (warehousing, agriculture). GEODNET ARR doubles to $14M. Auki CEX listing triggers re-rating. BitRobot and OpenMind TGE in 2026. XMAQUINA delivers first portfolio distribution event.

BEAR — 3 Years

Hardware production delays. AI embodiment problem harder than projected. Regulatory friction in key markets. Pre-TGE projects (BitRobot, OpenMind, XMAQUINA) face difficult launch environments. Only GEODNET and Auki retain value via revenue.

Across all three scenarios, GEODNET and Auki are the most defensible positions — both have real revenue, real customers, and network effects that compound with hardware proliferation regardless of crypto market conditions. XMAQUINA offers a structurally differentiated instrument with no liquid equivalent. The pre-TGE plays (BitRobot, OpenMind) carry higher variance but match the profile of early-stage venture bets with asymmetric upside.

Software ate the digital world. Embodied AI is about to eat the physical one. The infrastructure for that transition — positioning, spatial context, coordination, evaluation, capital access — is being built in public markets right now, at valuations that assume the hardware never comes.
